# Title: I Can’t Believe It’s Not Better: Pitfalls and Failure Modes in Vision-Language and BEV-Based Autonomous Driving
## Keywords
vision-language models, BEV perception, self-driving, vision-language action (VLA), LLM, CLIP, BLIP, LoRA, QLoRA, fine-tuning, contrastive learning, CoOp, Qwen-VL, negative results, failure modes, robustness, real-world deployment

## TL;DR
Despite rapid progress in vision-language models (VLMs), BEV perception, and LLM-based reasoning for autonomous driving, real-world deployment consistently reveals unexpected failures, robustness gaps, and generalization limits. This workshop focuses on negative results, reproducibility issues, and understudied failure modes across VLM–BEV–VLA pipelines for self-driving.

## Abstract
The I Can’t Believe It’s Not Better (ICBINB) workshop series advocates for transparent science, open discussion of negative and surprising results, and collective learning from failure. In recent years, vision-language models (VLMs), bird’s-eye-view (BEV) perception, vision-language-action (VLA) models, and large language models (LLMs) have reshaped the landscape of autonomous driving research. Architectures built on CLIP, BLIP, Qwen-VL, and similar foundations, combined with BEV feature fusion, contrastive learning, prompt learning (CoOp), and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA), have achieved strong performance on standard datasets and closed-loop simulation benchmarks. These advances have fueled optimism about end-to-end, reasoning-aware, and generalizable self-driving systems.

However, real-world deployment exposes critical and often underreported limitations: catastrophic distribution shifts, weak spatial-temporal consistency, hallucinatory reasoning, poor cross-scene generalization, unstable fine-tuning dynamics, and silent failures in safety-critical driving scenarios. Many state-of-the-art VLMs and BEV methods perform well in controlled settings but degrade sharply under weather variations, sensor noise, rare traffic events, or out-of-distribution inputs. Similarly, contrastive learning, prompt tuning, and lightweight fine-tuning strategies frequently yield unstable gains, inconsistent reproducibility, and hidden biases that are not captured by standard metrics.

This workshop seeks to uncover the **common failure modes, surprising negative results, and practical challenges** in applying VLMs, BEV perception, LLMs, and VLA systems to real-world self-driving. We invite submissions and discussions spanning algorithmic brittleness, evaluation pitfalls, dataset biases, optimization instabilities, safety risks, and deployment bottlenecks across both academic and industrial settings. By bringing together researchers in autonomous driving, computer vision, vision-language learning, and robotics, we aim to identify shared patterns of failure, highlight reproducibility issues, and foster cross-domain insights.

Ultimately, we hope to build a more robust and reliable foundation for vision–language–driving systems by openly confronting limitations, learning from unsuccessful attempts, and translating negative results into principled solutions. Embedding such transparency into research practice will accelerate the development of safe, generalizable, and trustworthy autonomous driving.
